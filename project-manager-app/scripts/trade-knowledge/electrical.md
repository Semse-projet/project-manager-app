# Electrical Work — SEMSE Trade Knowledge Guide

## Trade: electrical
## Visibility: public_training

---

## SAFETY REQUIREMENTS — NON-NEGOTIABLE

Before starting any electrical work, the following safety steps are mandatory:

1. **De-energize the circuit**: Turn off the breaker for the circuit you are working on. Lock the panel with a lockout/tagout device if available.
2. **Verify voltage is zero**: Use a non-contact voltage tester (NCVT) or a multimeter to confirm no voltage is present on all conductors before touching any wire.
3. **Post warning signs**: Place a sign on the breaker panel: "DO NOT ENERGIZE — WORK IN PROGRESS."
4. **Use PPE**: Wear insulated gloves (Class 00 minimum), safety glasses, and non-conductive footwear when working near live panels.
5. **Never work alone**: If you must work near live circuits above 50V, have a second person present who can call 911.
6. **GFCI protection required**: Always use GFCI-protected outlets in bathrooms, kitchens, garages, exterior, crawl spaces, and within 6 feet of sinks (NEC 210.8).

## CODE COMPLIANCE CHECKPOINTS (NEC 2023 / Local AHJ)

### Wiring Methods
- Romex (NM-B cable) is allowed in dry, protected locations inside walls — NOT exposed in garages or basements below 7 feet.
- In exposed locations in garages, basements, or commercial: use EMT conduit or MC cable.
- Minimum wire gauge for 15A circuits: 14 AWG. For 20A: 12 AWG. For 30A (dryer/HVAC): 10 AWG.
- All wire connections must be inside listed electrical boxes. No wire-to-wire splices in walls without a junction box.

### Box Fill Calculations
- Each 14 AWG wire counts as 2.0 cubic inches. Each 12 AWG counts as 2.25 cubic inches.
- Devices (receptacles, switches) count as 2 conductors of the largest wire.
- Ground wires: all grounds count as one conductor of the largest wire.
- Standard single-gang box (18 cu in) allows maximum 9 × 14 AWG wires.

### Circuit Requirements
- Kitchen: minimum 2 × 20A small appliance circuits for countertop receptacles (NEC 210.11).
- Bathroom: dedicated 20A circuit for GFCI receptacles (NEC 210.11(C)(3)).
- Laundry room: dedicated 20A circuit (NEC 210.52(F)).
- Bedroom circuits: AFCI protection required on all 15A and 20A circuits (NEC 210.12).
- Garage: GFCI outlets required, separate circuit recommended.
- Outdoor receptacles: GFCI + weatherproof covers rated "In-Use" (bubble covers).

### Panel Requirements
- Main panel must have a working main breaker disconnect.
- All breakers must be labeled accurately.
- Neutral and ground buses must be bonded ONLY at the main panel, NOT at sub-panels.
- Sub-panels require separate neutral and ground bars.
- Minimum 6 ft of headroom in front of the panel (NEC 110.26).
- No combustible storage within 3 feet of the panel.

## COMMON PROCEDURES

### Installing a New Outlet (Receptacle)
1. Turn off breaker. Verify with tester.
2. Pull existing wire through new box location or run new NM-B cable from source.
3. Strip 3/4" of insulation from each conductor (hot=black, neutral=white, ground=bare/green).
4. Connect: black to brass screw (hot side), white to silver screw (neutral side), ground to green screw.
5. Push into box, mount cover plate.
6. Turn on breaker, test with outlet tester (verify correct polarity and ground).

### Installing a GFCI Outlet
1. Same process as above, but use GFCI receptacle.
2. Connect incoming "LINE" wires to LINE terminals (labeled).
3. If protecting downstream outlets, connect downstream wires to LOAD terminals.
4. Press TEST button — outlet should lose power. Press RESET — outlet should restore power.
5. Label downstream outlets "GFCI Protected."

### Adding a Circuit Breaker
1. Main breaker must be OFF. Verify with tester that bus bars are de-energized.
2. Install correct breaker type/brand for your panel (tandem/full-size as required).
3. Connect: hot wire to breaker terminal, neutral to neutral bus, ground to ground bus.
4. Torque breaker terminal to spec (usually 35 in-lbs for 15-20A breakers).
5. Label breaker in panel directory.

### Rough-In Wiring for New Room
1. Plan circuits: bedrooms (AFCI), kitchen (GFCI + 20A), baths (GFCI + 20A), general lighting.
2. Run cables through studs, drill 1-1/8" holes centered in 2x4 studs.
3. Install nail plates wherever cable passes within 1-1/4" of stud face (NEC 300.4).
4. Staple NM-B cable within 8" of every box and every 4.5 ft along runs.
5. Leave 8" of wire sticking out from each box opening.
6. Have rough-in inspected BEFORE closing walls.

## INSPECTION CHECKPOINTS

### Rough-In Inspection (before drywall)
- [ ] All boxes secured to framing (no floating boxes).
- [ ] Box fill not exceeded.
- [ ] AFCI breakers installed where required (bedrooms, living areas).
- [ ] GFCI locations correct (kitchen, bath, exterior, garage).
- [ ] Nail plates installed where required.
- [ ] Cables secured with staples or straps.
- [ ] Panel wiring neat, conductors labeled.
- [ ] Grounding electrode system complete (ground rod, Ufer, or water pipe per NEC 250).
- [ ] Dedicated circuits for high-draw appliances (dishwasher, refrigerator, microwave).

### Final Inspection (after drywall and finish)
- [ ] All boxes have covers or devices installed — no open boxes.
- [ ] GFCI outlets test correctly with GFCI tester.
- [ ] AFCI breakers installed and not tripping nuisance trips.
- [ ] Outlet polarity correct in all receptacles (use 3-light tester).
- [ ] Panel labeled completely and accurately.
- [ ] Smoke detectors on each floor and in each bedroom (hardwired, interconnected per NEC 760).
- [ ] CO detector within 15 feet of sleeping rooms if gas appliances present.
- [ ] Outdoor receptacles have in-use weatherproof covers.

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

1. **Overloading circuits**: Never put more than 80% load on a circuit. A 20A circuit = max 16A continuous.
2. **Wrong wire gauge**: Using 14 AWG on a 20A circuit is a fire hazard. Always match wire to breaker.
3. **Shared neutral problems**: Never share a neutral between two circuits on the same leg — causes overheating. Exception: MWBC (Multi-Wire Branch Circuit) with handle-tied breakers on opposite legs.
4. **Loose connections**: Hand-tighten isn't enough. Torque all terminals to manufacturer spec. Loose connections cause arcing and fires.
5. **Aluminum wiring**: If you find aluminum wiring (dull silver color), treat as a special case. Use CO/ALR-rated devices or pigtail with copper using listed connectors (Ideal 65 purple wire nuts or AlumiConn connectors).
6. **Open junction boxes in walls**: Every splice must be in an accessible, covered junction box. No exceptions.
7. **Reversed polarity**: Always verify black=hot, white=neutral with a tester before finishing.
8. **Ground loops in sub-panels**: In sub-panels, neutral and ground MUST be separated — do not bond them together.

## TROUBLESHOOTING GUIDE

### Breaker trips immediately
- Cause: short circuit (hot touching ground or neutral directly).
- Check: look for pinched wires in outlets, damaged cable jackets, incorrect wiring at device.

### GFCI trips randomly
- Cause: moisture intrusion, long cable runs picking up ground leakage, old wiring with insulation degradation.
- Fix: check for actual moisture, measure leakage current with clamp meter, replace aging devices.

### AFCI trips randomly
- Cause: legitimate arc in wiring, or nuisance trip from older appliances (vacuum motors, older dimmers).
- Fix: check wiring for damage; if appliance causes trip, wire that device on a standard circuit if code allows.

### No power at outlet
- Check: breaker is on, GFCI upstream has not tripped (press RESET), wire connections at device are tight.

### Lights flickering
- Cause: loose neutral connection — the most dangerous fault type (can cause voltage surges).
- Action: find and tighten neutral at every connection point from the outlet to the panel. This is urgent.

## TRADE-SPECIFIC BILLING NOTES FOR ESTIMATING

- Rough-in wiring: price per outlet/switch rough-in point (~1.5–2.5 hrs per point including cable run).
- Panel upgrade (100A to 200A): typically 6–10 hrs labor + materials.
- GFCI outlet install: 30–60 min per outlet.
- Dedicated circuit add: 2–4 hrs (run from panel to device location).
- Smoke/CO detector installation: 30–45 min per hardwired unit.
- Service entrance / meter base: requires utility coordination; allow 2–3 week lead time.
