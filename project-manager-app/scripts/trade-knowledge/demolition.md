## OVERVIEW — DEMOLITION IS DISCOVERY, NOT JUST DESTRUCTION

Demolition is the phase where most hidden conditions are revealed. Every scope change that originates in demo must be:
1. Photographed immediately.
2. Communicated to the client in writing before proceeding.
3. Priced as a change order with a signed approval before work continues.

A contractor who proceeds with remediation work after discovering hidden conditions without client approval has no enforceable claim for that work in most states.

## PRE-DEMOLITION SAFETY

### Utility Shutoffs — Non-Negotiable
1. **Electric**: Turn off circuit breakers for all circuits in the demo area. Use a non-contact voltage tester to verify EVERY wire before touching. Turn off main breaker if full demo.
2. **Gas**: Close gas shutoff valve at appliance and verify by feeling the line while attempting ignition. If extensive demo near gas lines: close street-side valve and call gas company.
3. **Water**: Shut off supply valves at fixtures. If pipes will be cut, shut off the main water supply and drain lines.
4. **HVAC**: Disconnect and cap any ducts in the demo zone. Cover registers in adjacent areas.
5. **Call 811**: Required before ANY ground penetration. Even interior concrete slab cuts may encounter in-slab conduit. Call minimum 3 business days before.

### Hazardous Materials Assessment
- **Lead paint**: Assume present in homes built before 1978. Do not sand, scrape, heat, or disturb coated surfaces without testing first. EPA RRP rules apply if >6 sq ft interior or >20 sq ft exterior.
- **Asbestos**: Assume present in floor tiles (9"×9" vinyl), pipe insulation, boiler/furnace wrap, roofing felt, exterior siding (cement fiber/transite), ceiling texture (before 1980). Test before cutting, drilling, or disturbing.
- **Vermiculite insulation**: In attics of pre-1990 homes, often contains asbestos. Do not disturb without testing.
- **Mold**: Use N95 minimum when cutting into walls where mold is possible (chronic moisture areas). Mold visible in demo → stop, document, assess scope before proceeding.

### PPE Requirements for Demo
- **Eyes**: Safety glasses at minimum; goggles if overhead work.
- **Respiratory**: N95 for drywall, wood, and plaster dust; P100 half-face for mold or suspected asbestos; full-face respirator for confirmed asbestos abatement.
- **Hands**: Work gloves for general demo; puncture-resistant gloves when working near sharp debris.
- **Feet**: Steel-toed boots — demo floors contain nails and sharp debris.
- **Head**: Hard hat for any overhead demolition.
- **Hearing**: Protection when operating demo hammer, reciprocating saw, or jackhammer.

## SITE PROTECTION

### Protecting Adjacent Areas
- **Dust containment**: hang 6-mil poly sheeting from floor to ceiling across all doorways into demo area. Tape to walls and floor. Negative pressure with a box fan or air scrubber exhausting outdoors (reduces dust migration 80–90%).
- **Floor protection**: cover all non-demolition floors with masonite panels (not plastic — plastic slides and tears). Overlap panels 6" and tape. Protect stairways with cardboard.
- **Fixture protection**: if keeping existing fixtures (bathtub, toilet, cabinets), cover them with drop cloths or poly sheeting before demo begins.
- **Exterior**: in multi-story or window-adjacent demo, hang debris netting or catch screens. Alert neighbors when demo debris will be near shared property lines.

### Air Filtration
- Use a HEPA air scrubber in the demo area for any demolition lasting more than 2 hours.
- HEPA filtration captures particles as small as 0.3 microns — captures drywall dust, mold spores, and most construction particulates.
- Change pre-filter every day and HEPA filter per manufacturer schedule.

## STRUCTURAL VS NON-STRUCTURAL DEMOLITION

**This is the most critical decision in demo. Getting it wrong causes structural failure.**

### Non-Load-Bearing Walls (Safe to Remove)
- Indicators: run parallel to joists overhead, do not continue from floor to floor in multi-story buildings, no posts or beams above.
- To confirm: look in attic or basement — if joists run parallel to the wall above, the wall is likely non-structural.
- Even with indicators, always verify with a structural engineer before removing any wall that you are not 100% certain is non-load-bearing.
- Interior partition walls, closet walls, most bathroom dividers: typically non-load-bearing.

### Load-Bearing Walls (NEVER Remove Without a Plan)
- Indicators: run perpendicular to joists, aligned with posts or columns in basement, centered in the house, continuation of walls on multiple floors.
- If removing: requires an engineer's beam size calculation, temporary shoring during demo, beam installation, and post-beam-to-foundation load path verification.
- Temporary shoring must be designed and installed BEFORE any load-bearing wall is cut or removed.
- Do not estimate — hire an engineer. The cost is $300–$800. A collapsed floor costs $50,000+.

### Shoring During Demo
- For any load-bearing element removal: install temporary walls (3/4" plywood top plate + 2×4 studs at 16" OC) under the ceiling joists on both sides of the wall being removed.
- Temporary walls must bear on the subfloor (not floating), and the subfloor must have adequate bearing below.
- Keep shoring in place until the permanent beam is fully installed and bearing.

## SELECTIVE DEMOLITION PROCEDURES

### Drywall Removal
1. Score around area with a utility knife first (prevents drywall paper from tearing in unintended areas).
2. Use a drywall saw or reciprocating saw with short blade to cut between studs.
3. Remove drywall screws or nails — do not pull panels with nails still in the wall (tears backing paper, damages studs).
4. Save reusable sections if patching adjacent areas.

### Tile Removal
- **Floor tile**: angle grinder or rotary hammer with chisel attachment. Work at angle — hit the grout line first, not the center of the tile (to save the substrate if reusing).
- **Wall tile (in shower)**: oscillating tool to cut grout; pry tile off. Expect cement board damage.
- **For re-tile**: if cement board is intact and undamaged, it can often be reused. If damaged or soft, replace completely.

### Flooring Removal
- Vinyl or laminate: pull corners, pry up strips. Remove adhesive with floor scraper.
- Hardwood: pry up with flooring bar, working with the grain.
- Carpet: cut into 3-foot strips with a carpet knife, roll and remove. Remove tack strips and staples completely — staples left in floor will puncture new flooring.

### Cabinet Removal
- Remove all drawers and doors first — reduces weight and prevents damage to client's salvageable items.
- Upper cabinets: unscrew from wall studs (screws through hanging rail). Support cabinet while removing last 2 screws — upper cabinets can fall.
- Base cabinets: remove countertop first. Unscrew toe kick, unscrew cabinet from wall.
- Preserve if client requests salvage.

### Concrete (Slab)
- Mark cut lines with chalk or pencil. Cover all HVAC, plumbing, and electrical registers.
- Diamond blade on a circular saw or angle grinder for cuts. Water cool if possible (reduces silica dust enormously).
- Rotary hammer with chisel or jackhammer for breakout.
- **Silica dust**: wear P100 half-face respirator. Concrete cutting produces respirable crystalline silica — highest risk activity in construction. Water cooling + HEPA vacuum at cutting head are best controls.
- After breakout: check for rebar, conduit, or pipes before further cutting.

## DEBRIS HANDLING

### Site Management
- Designate a debris staging area at the start of the project — do not scatter debris through the house.
- Clear debris daily (or every half-day for heavy demo). Piled debris is a trip hazard and a fire hazard.
- Separate salvageable materials (client's property or resellable items) from waste.

### Dumpster Logistics
- Size: 10-yard dumpster for single room; 20-yard for full first-floor; 30-yard for full house.
- Place on street only with permit (most municipalities). On driveway: protect with plywood sheets under dumpster corners.
- Weight limits: mixed construction debris averages 1,000–1,500 lbs per cubic yard. Tile and concrete weigh more — warn client about overweight charges.

### Hazardous Waste
- **Asbestos**: bagged in 6-mil labeled poly bags, disposed at licensed facility only.
- **Lead paint**: encapsulated in bags or sealed containers. Follow EPA RRP disposal requirements.
- **Fluorescent tubes**: contain mercury — do not break. Dispose at hazardous waste facility.
- **Roofing materials with asbestos content**: double-bag, disposed as regulated waste.
- **Paint cans**: let dry completely, dispose as solid waste (dry paint not hazardous in most states).

## PHOTO DOCUMENTATION PROTOCOL

Good demo documentation protects the contractor from false damage claims and supports change orders.

### Required Photos
1. **Pre-demo existing conditions**: every wall, floor, and ceiling in scope — wide shot + detail.
2. **During demo — hidden conditions**: mold, rot, structural issues, electrical deficiencies, plumbing problems. Photograph before ANY remediation begins.
3. **Structural elements**: any beam, joist, or post exposed during demo — condition and configuration.
4. **Completed demo**: all surfaces clean, ready for rough-in phase.

### Photo Timing
- Take photos before starting each day.
- Take photos before covering or removing any discovered condition.
- Never delete site photos from a project — even "boring" photos may become evidence in a dispute.

## CHANGE ORDER TRIGGERS — DEMOLITION PHASE

These discoveries require a stop, document, and change-order process:

| Discovery | Action | Typical Cost Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Load-bearing wall (thought to be non-bearing) | Stop, hire engineer, get signed CO | $800–$5,000+ |
| Subfloor rot or damage | Stop, photograph, scope replacement | $400–$3,000 |
| Active mold (black or extensive) | Stop, test, remediation plan | $500–$10,000+ |
| Asbestos-containing materials | Stop, test, hire abatement contractor | $800–$5,000+ |
| Out-of-spec existing plumbing | Document, price replacement in CO | $200–$2,000 |
| Knob-and-tube wiring (active) | Stop, electrician assessment | $1,500–$10,000 |
| Structural damage (rot, insect damage) | Stop, engineer assessment | $1,000–$20,000+ |
| Inadequate existing electrical | Document, CO for upgrade | $600–$3,000 |

## INSPECTION AND CLOSEOUT CHECKLIST

### During Demo
- [ ] All utilities verified off before starting work each day.
- [ ] Dust containment in place (poly sheeting, negative pressure if applicable).
- [ ] PPE worn by all workers.
- [ ] Hidden conditions documented before remediation.
- [ ] Client notified of hidden conditions in writing before proceeding.
- [ ] Structural work (shoring) in place if load-bearing elements are affected.

### End of Demo Phase
- [ ] All debris removed to staging area or dumpster.
- [ ] Subfloor swept and cleared of nails, screws, staples.
- [ ] Walls cleared of drywall residue at fastener locations.
- [ ] All utility stubs capped (plumbing, electrical, HVAC).
- [ ] All windows and doors secured if exterior is exposed.
- [ ] Photos of completed demo scope documented.
- [ ] Change orders signed for all discovered hidden conditions.
- [ ] Site is safe to leave for the day (no trip hazards, electrical hazards, or open penetrations).

## SAFETY CHECKLIST — START OF EVERY DEMO DAY

- [ ] Confirm breakers are off. Voltage-test before picking up tools.
- [ ] Gas shutoff confirmed off (if gas appliances in area).
- [ ] Water main off if pipes are open.
- [ ] Dust containment intact from previous day.
- [ ] PPE on before entering demo zone.
- [ ] First aid kit on site and location known by all crew.
- [ ] Emergency contacts posted.
