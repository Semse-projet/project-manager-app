# Carpentry Work — SEMSE Trade Knowledge Guide

## Trade: carpentry
## Visibility: public_training

---

## SAFETY REQUIREMENTS

1. **Power tool safety**: Guards must be in place on all saws. Never remove blade guards. Unplug tools before changing blades or bits.
2. **Eye protection**: Safety glasses are mandatory when cutting, routing, nailing, or operating any power tool.
3. **Hearing protection**: Circular saws, routers, and nail guns exceed 90 dB. Use hearing protection for extended operation.
4. **Dust**: Wood dust (especially hardwoods like oak, walnut, and cherry) is a known carcinogen. N95 minimum for finish work; dust collection system for sustained production.
5. **Lifting**: Engineered lumber (LVL beams) can exceed 200 lbs for long spans. Plan crane lifts or have adequate crew.
6. **Fall protection**: Framing on second-floor decks, floor joists, and roof work requires fall protection at 6 feet above lower level on residential, 4 feet in construction.
7. **Nail gun**: Never bypass the contact safety tip. Keep finger off trigger when not firing. Keep free hand clear of work being nailed.

## CODE COMPLIANCE CHECKPOINTS (IRC 2021)

### Framing Requirements
- Stud spacing: 16" OC for load-bearing walls; 24" OC allowed for non-load-bearing interior partitions in some designs.
- Double top plate required on load-bearing walls.
- Headers over openings: size depends on span and load. Double 2×10 spans up to 4 ft in most cases; engineered lumber (LVL) required for longer spans or high loads.
- Cripple studs above and below headers.
- Corner framing: minimum 3-stud corners (California corner provides backing for insulation — preferred).
- Fire blocking required: at every floor level, at 10-foot vertical intervals in walls, in concealed cavities above dropped ceilings.
- Blocking between studs at mid-height of walls over 10 feet.

### Floor Framing
- Joist span tables per species and grade (use AWC Span Tables or IRC Appendix B).
- Joists must bear minimum 1.5" on wood, 3" on masonry.
- Double joists under parallel partition walls.
- Block or cross-bridge floor joists per span table (typically at 8-foot intervals).
- Notches and holes: notch max 1/6 of joist depth in outer 1/3 of span; hole max 1/3 joist depth at center 1/3 of span; minimum 2" from edge.
- LVL and I-joists: no field notching or cutting flanges — strictly per manufacturer specs.

### Roof Framing
- Ridge board must be minimum depth of rafter plumb cut.
- Collar ties (every other pair at upper third of rafter) — not to be confused with rafter ties at wall plate.
- Hurricane straps/rafter ties at every rafter-to-wall plate connection in high-wind zones.
- Snow loads: consult local design values (30–100 PSF ground snow load varies significantly by region and elevation).

## COMMON PROCEDURES

### Framing a Non-Load-Bearing Wall
1. Determine wall layout. Snap chalk line on floor, plumb with level to ceiling.
2. Cut bottom plate (pressure-treated if within 18" of concrete slab on grade, or any plate in contact with concrete).
3. Mark stud layout from one end: 15-1/4", then every 16" from that point (ensures sheathing edges hit at 16" OC).
4. Cut all studs to height (wall height minus 3× plate thickness = 3× 1.5" = 4.5").
5. Assemble flat on floor: nail top and bottom plates to studs with 2 nails each end (16d sinker nails).
6. Tip up, align to chalk line, plumb in both directions, nail to floor and ceiling.
7. Add blocking at 48" height for handrail backing, TV brackets, or heavy fixtures.

### Framing a Door Opening
1. Determine header size (verify with load calculation or engineer for load-bearing).
2. King studs: full-height studs on each side of opening.
3. Jack studs (trimmers): support header on each side; height = door height + 1-1/2" for frame + 3/4" for clearance = rough opening height.
4. Header: LVL or doubled dimensional lumber with 1/2" OSB spacer to match wall width.
5. Cripple studs above header to top plate at 16" OC.
6. Rough opening width: door width + 2" for frame + 1/4" clearance each side.
7. Rough opening height: door height + 2" for frame + 1" for flooring clearance.

### Installing Hardwood Flooring
1. Acclimate flooring 3–7 days in room at installation temperature and humidity.
2. Check subfloor: must be flat to 3/16" in 10 feet, dry (< 12% MC), structurally sound. Fasten any squeaky boards with screws.
3. Establish straight reference line 6" from starting wall (snap chalk line).
4. Starter row: face nail through face, then blind nail through tongue.
5. Subsequent rows: blind nail (15-16 gauge cleat nailer) through tongue at 45°, every 6–8 inches along each board.
6. End joints must be offset minimum 6 inches between adjacent rows.
7. Leave 3/4" expansion gap at all walls and vertical obstructions.
8. Last row: face nail or use pull bar to click into place, face nail.
9. Install shoe molding or base to cover expansion gap.

### Installing Exterior Doors and Windows (Rough Opening)
1. Check rough opening for square: measure both diagonals — must match within 1/8".
2. Check level at sill and plumb at both sides.
3. For windows: apply 6" wide self-adhering flashing membrane around rough opening (sill first, then sides lapping over sill, then head lapping over sides — pan flashing principle).
4. Set unit in opening. Level, plumb, and square before fastening.
5. Shim as needed — shimming at hinge and strike locations on doors.
6. Nail through flange (windows/prehung doors) at required spacing per manufacturer.
7. Flash exterior: integrate with water-resistive barrier (WRB) above — "upper flap goes over, lower goes under."
8. Insulate gap between frame and rough opening with low-expansion spray foam or fiberglass batt.
9. Trim interior, adjust hinges, verify door swings freely and latches correctly.

### Installing Base and Casing Trim
1. Cut base returns at outside corners with compound miter.
2. Cope inside corners: cut first piece square into corner; second piece coped (cut profile on front face at 45°, back-cut slightly).
3. Sand coped profile with 120-grit before test-fitting.
4. Nail with 15-gauge finish nailer: into base (near bottom, into plate) and near top into drywall/stud.
5. Caulk inside corners, tops of base, and butt joints with paintable caulk.
6. Fill nail holes with wood filler after painting.

## INSPECTION CHECKPOINTS

### Framing Inspection (before sheathing or insulation)
- [ ] All beams and headers properly sized and bearing on full jack studs.
- [ ] Rough openings correct dimensions (door and window sizes verified with RO schedule).
- [ ] Fire blocking installed at all required locations.
- [ ] Double top plates lapped at corners and intersections.
- [ ] Engineered lumber installed per manufacturer specs (no field cuts on I-joists).
- [ ] Hurricane ties/rafter ties installed at all rafter-to-plate connections.
- [ ] Blocking at mid-span of floor joists.
- [ ] Pressure-treated lumber in all required locations (sill plates, bottom plates on slab).
- [ ] Anchor bolts embedded correctly in foundation (1/2" × 10" minimum, 6 feet OC max, 12" from corners).

### Finish Carpentry Inspection (after installation)
- [ ] Doors open and close freely without binding.
- [ ] Door latches and locks function correctly.
- [ ] Trim joints tight at corners (no gaps over 1/16").
- [ ] Flooring gaps consistent and within spec.
- [ ] No squeaks in floor (walk entire floor systematically).
- [ ] Expansion gaps correct at walls.
- [ ] Cabinets plumb, level, and secured to studs.

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

1. **Crown the wrong way on joists**: Crown (natural bow) of floor joists must face UP — load straightens them. Crown down causes sag.
2. **Skipping fire blocking**: Open stud cavities create concealed fire paths. Horizontal 2×4 blocking every 10 ft is code-required.
3. **Undersized headers**: Rule of thumb is not code compliance. Size headers by load calculation or use span tables.
4. **Not checking diagonals on rough openings**: An out-of-square rough opening means fighting to install every door and window. Square it during framing, not after.
5. **Face-nailing hardwood flooring throughout**: Only starter row is face-nailed. All other rows blind-nail through tongue.
6. **No expansion gap in flooring**: Wood expands and contracts seasonally. Flooring without expansion gaps will buckle and cup.
7. **Bottom plate not pressure-treated on slab**: Concrete wicks moisture. Non-PT lumber rots within 3–5 years.
8. **Notching I-joists or LVL**: Engineered lumber must not be notched or have holes outside of manufacturer specs — causes immediate structural failure.
